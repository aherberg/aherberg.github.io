Crunchbase

Knowledge Center usability study.

Crunchbase is a leading platform for professionals to discover innovative companies, connect with the people behind them, and uncover new opportunities. Over 50 million professionals use the product including founders, entrepreneurs, investors, market researchers, and salespeople to inform their business decisions.

In partnership with Crunchbase.com, my team and I evaluated the information architecture of Crunchbase‚Äôs Knowledge Center–their self-service support site. There‚Äôs little data assessing the efficacy of user experiences of the Knowledge Center and its navigation logic. Because of this, we investigated if the intended purpose of the Knowledge Center achieves its goal to provide an efficient product to help users understand Crunchbase‚Äôs services and offerings.

The project concluded with a presentation to Crunchbase‚Äôs Customer Experience (CX) team. You can view our final presentation here.

Research Questions

- Are Knowledge Center articles categorized similar to users‚Äô perceptions?

- Can users successfully navigate to specific content in the Knowledge Center?

- What options do users want to see when browsing the Knowledge Center?

- Can users recover from errors they encounter while navigating the Knowledge Center?

Process

Heuristic Evaluation

After conducting a heuristic evaluation with the team, we identified two major areas of opportunity to understand user pain points with the Knowledge Center: 1) Topics & categories and 2) terminologies.

Methods

Working alongside Crunchbase‚Äôs UX Research team, we used two research methods, a card sorting exercise and a tree test, respectively. In total, 58 participants contributed to the study, with 7 of those completing the second stage of the study (the tree test).

Hybrid Card Sort (Unmoderated) Tree Test (Moderated)

Card Sort

The purpose was to document how people organize article titles into predetermined categories, and to what extent they would create their own categories to align with their own mental models. After identifying outliers and standardizing the created categories, a total of 58 participants were included in our analysis.

Participants were presented with 48 article titles chosen by Crunchbase‚Äôs research team, along with 10 pre-made categories based on current Knowledge Center categories (+ ‚ÄúOther‚Äù category)

During the exercise, participants drag & dropped articles into categories based on where they thought they‚Äôd be located

Tree Test

Following the card sorting exercise, we identified 7 target users from the participant pool to complete the tree test (3 founders, 2 marketers, 1 investor, 1 salesperson). We recorded moderated walkthrough sessions with the 7 participants (individually) and presented them with 5 scenario-based tasks to find specific article titles in a set of categories based on results from the card sorting exercise.

Participants were presented with 10 categories containing articles, organized to reflect participant categorization from the card sort. During the exercise, participants were given a specific article to discover, then navigated the tree to find the article based on their perceptions

Analysis

For our analysis, we identified different types of quantitative and qualitative data from each stage of the study. For the card sort, we standardized participants‚Äô responses to reorganize articles to align with the mental models detected after the exercise. For the tree test, we used a mix of quantitative and qualitative methods to confirm mental models, detect patterns, and understand users‚Äô thought processes.

Findings

Overall, the results of our study found that users rely on key terms and words in article titles to dictate their navigation of categories and vague terminologies in article titles impacted users‚Äô ability to distinguish its meaning and relevance to existing categories. For our findings, we highlighted 2 high-severity articles and themes that inconvenienced users.

High-Severity Callouts

01. Article: ‚ÄúWhere does Crunchbase get their data?‚Äù

Broad, ambiguous terminologies made it difficult for users to understand an article‚Äôs context, so they relied on keywords to guide their decision. For this article, there was a clear disconnect. In the Knowledge Center, it is located in the ‚ÄúGet to know Crunchbase‚Äù category, however, users associated the article with User Data & Privacy and Crunchbase Data Partners.

02. Article: ‚ÄúHow much does Crunchbase cost?‚Äù

Similar to the article about data, the article about Crunchbase‚Äôs general cost required more critical thinking from participants. The article is curently located in the ‚ÄúGet to know Crunchbase‚Äù category, however, users expressed multiple options that seemed relevant to the question: Payments & Billing, Pro Subscription, Enterprise Subscription, and Get to know Crunchbase. The most common error was participants defaulting to Payments & Billing because they associated ‚Äúcost‚Äù with ‚Äúpayments‚Äù.

Affinity Mapping

To further visualize our findings, we created an affinity map to better understand participants‚Äô mental models and patterns in behavior. We identified patterns through quotes and observed reactions the users displayed during the tree test.

Design Recommendations

Based on our study, we recommended that the Customer Experience team should develop Crunchbase-specific terminologies to reduce the use of vague, or broad, wording (i.e. ‚Äúdata‚Äù, ‚Äúintegration‚Äù, or ‚Äúcost‚Äù). Specific terminologies could help users differentiate the content of an article based on the title.

Additionally, we suggested that certain topics are ‚Äúbundled‚Äù together rather than having separate categories which caused overlap and redundancies. Most importantly, a category covering all subscriptions rather than individually since users had a difficult time understanding the differences in subscriptions and pricing.

Limitations

This study had plenty of limitations. Aside from the obvious (COVID pandemic and 100% remote communication), remote interviews restricted our ability to analyze physical behavior in participants. Another limitation was the depth of the study as well; we only tested the Knowledge Center via a simulator and never had the chance to test users‚Äô behavior on the actual site. Using the actual site would have made for an entirely different discoverability exercise since the actual Knowledge Center has a search bar and more appealing layout than a basic list of items.

Learnings

For this study, I would have approached the problem differently and focused more on the article titles and how people perceived them as well as making sure they were categorized logically. I learned that there needs to be diversity in data points because our study only painted a small portion of a larger problem to be solved, which may have been due to our methods. I also learned the importance of screening participants to protect data integrity.

Moreover, as a team lead, I learned a lot about how to organize and prioritize a project. I learned it is just as important to make sure everyone is on the same page as it is to make sure you're adhering to a restricted timeline. Gaps in communication can set you back weeks if not done efficiently.

What's Next?

When presenting our study, we suggested that next steps for the CX team should be evaluating the content of articles, as well as doing a thorough competitive analysis to better understand how their competitors are helping their users via self-service support pages.
